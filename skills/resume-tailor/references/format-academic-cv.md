# Format: Academic / Research-Institute CV

Use this for **universities, research institutes, AI/ML institutes (Amii,
Vector, Mila), national labs, PI/research/fellowship/scientist roles**, and
academic-adjacent education/training roles. These employers read **academic
CVs**, not 2-page industry resumes. A terse résumé here actively sells the
candidate short — it hides publications, funding, talks, and software that are
the currency of credibility.

## Target
A **clean, full ~3-page CV** (US Letter, Awesome-CV class). Full work history,
plus the academic sections below. Fill the third page properly — an academic CV
that trails off at 30% of page 3 looks unfinished (see page-fill discipline in
SKILL.md step 6).

## Section order (build by copying the closest existing variant, then adapt)
1. **Summary** — 4–6 lines; lead with the credentials that matter here
   (PhD/degree, publications, funding, teaching, the specific ML/AI/research
   fit). Name the institute's mission if genuine.
2. **Professional Experience** — full reverse-chronological history, tailored
   bullets. Keep AI/research/technical leadership prominent.
3. **Teaching & Academic Leadership** — courses designed/taught (with course
   codes), graduate supervision, curriculum, mentorship. For an academic
   institute, graduate supervision is real *people leadership* and a defensible
   answer to "lead a team" requirements — frame it honestly (it is academic
   supervision, not corporate people-management).
4. **Invited Lectures & Workshops** — list talks individually (title, venue,
   place, year). Strong signal for any teaching/education role.
5. **Earlier Experience** — older roles, compact but present (never dropped).
6. **Skills** — ML/AI + technical, grouped; include a Programming Languages
   line to show breadth.
7. **Selected Software & Projects** — a substantial grouped list (AI/LLM,
   domain, tools); this is where a builder's real depth shows. Pull from the
   GitHub/local-project scan.
8. **Research Funding** — grants individually, with the candidate's **exact
   role** (Principal Investigator vs Co-applicant vs Additional Author — these
   differ and must match the source), funder, years, amount.
9. **Selected Publications** — list ~8–12 representative papers (title, journal,
   year) + the total count + an ORCID link. **ORCID is authoritative** — verify
   titles/journals against it, not against an older/incomplete `cv/publications`
   file. Use ORCID as the publications link if no Google Scholar profile exists.
10. **Professional Activities** — memberships, peer review, conference
    presentation count.
11. **Education** — degrees; note thesis if notable; equivalency line if the
    degree field differs from the posting's "CS/ML required" (graduate
    scientific-software training counts).

## Sourcing the academic sections
If the repo has a modular academic CV (e.g. `cv/*.tex`: `invited.tex`,
`funding.tex`, `publications.tex`, `software.tex`, `activities.tex`), read those
for the real, exact content — do not invent talks, grants, or papers. Keep
grant **roles** and **amounts** exactly as the source states.

## Honesty notes specific to academic CVs
- **Grant role accuracy:** PI ≠ Co-applicant ≠ Additional Author. Codex will
  cross-check against the funding source; get it right.
- **Publication accuracy:** verify each listed title/journal/year against ORCID.
- **"Lead a team" via supervision:** honest to present graduate-student
  supervision + mentorship + standards-setting as people leadership for an
  academic institute, but disclose it's academic/technical leadership, not
  years of formal corporate people-management, if the candidate lacks the
  latter.
- Same anti-fabrication gate and codex review as all formats.

## Cover letter
1 page, the candidate's voice. Lead with genuine mission fit, then the academic
credentials (teaching, publications, funding) + the technical/ML fit. If the
posting asks for a "proudest accomplishment" or similar, that is the
candidate's to name — ask them (see SKILL.md "ask, never guess"). Disclose any
honest gaps (e.g. a method the institute specializes in that the candidate is
eager to deepen) — turning a gap into mission-fit is stronger than hiding it.
